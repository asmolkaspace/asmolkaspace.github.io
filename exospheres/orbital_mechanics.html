Orbital Mechanics

The following functions are based on the following two works:

  • Walter, U., 2018. Astronautics, The Physics of Space Flight. 3 ed., Springer Cham.
  • N. Schörghofer, User guide, planetary-code-collection: Thermal and ice evolution models for planetary surfaces, 2002-2022

The general form of every function in this orbital mechanics library is <output-name>_<input-symbol-1>[_<input-symbol-2>][...]. Thus, there are multiple ways of calculating the desired output.

NameEquation(s)Function
Azimuth angle$\alpha(\textbf{v}) = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{v_2}{\sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}} \right)$azimuth_angle_v

Eccentric Anomaly$E_0(e, M) = M \\ E_{n+1}(e, M) = M + e \cdot \sin E_n$eccentric_anomaly_e_M
Eccentricity$e(f_2, \alpha) = \sqrt{1 - 4 \cdot f_2 \cdot (1 - f_2) \cdot \sin^2\alpha }$eccentricity_f2_alpha
Eccentricity$e(f_2, \textbf{v}) = e(f_2, \alpha(\textbf{v}))$eccentricity_f2_v
Eccentricity$e(r, m, \textbf{v}) = e(f_2(r, m, \textbf{v}), \alpha(\textbf{v}))$eccentricity_r_m_v
Escape velocity$v_{esc}(r, m)=\sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot G m}{r}}$escape_velocity_r_m
Squared $v_{esc}$ fraction$f_2(v_{esc}, \textbf{v}) = \left( \frac{|\textbf{v}|}{v_{esc}} \right)^2$fvesc2_vesc_v
Squared $v_{esc}$ fraction$f_2(r, m, \textbf{v}) = f_2(v_{esc}(r, m), \textbf{v})$fvesc2_r_m_v
Ground distance$d(R, d\theta) = d\theta \cdot R$ground_distance_R_dtheta
Ground distance$d(R, a, e) = d(R, \theta(R, a, e))$ground_distance_R_a_e
Mean anomaly$M(e, E) = E - e \cdot \sin E$mean_anomaly_e_E
Mean anomaly$M(e, \theta) = M(e, E(e, \theta))$mean_anomaly_e_theta
Orbital period$P(a, m) = 2\pi \cdot \sqrt{\frac{a^3}{Gm}}$orbit_period_a_m
Orbit time$t(M, P) = M \cdot \frac{P}{2\pi}$orbit_time_M_P
Orbit time$t(e, E, P) = t(M(e, E), P)$orbit_time_e_E_P
Orbit time$t(e, \theta, P) = t(e, E(e, \theta), P)$orbit_time_e_theta_P
Semi-latus rectum$p(a, e) = a \cdot (1 - e^2)$semi_latus_rectum_a_e
Semi-latus rectum$p(r, e, \theta) = r \cdot (1 + e \cdot \cos \theta)$semi_latus_rectum_r_e_theta
Semi-major axis$a(r, f_2) = \frac{r}{2 \cdot (1 - f_2)}$semi_major_axis_r_f2
Semi-major axis$a(r, m, \textbf{v}) = a(r, f_2(r, m, \textbf{v}))$semi_major_axis_r_m_v
Semi-major axis$a(p, e) = \frac{p}{1 - e^2}$semi_major_axis_p_e
Semi-major axis$a(r, e, \theta) = \frac{r \cdot (1 + e\cdot\cos\theta)}{1 - e^2}$semi_major_axis_r_e_theta
True anomaly$\theta(r, a, e) = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{e} \cdot \left( \frac{a}{r} \cdot (1 - e^2) - 1 \right) \right)$true_anomaly_r_a_e
True anomaly$\theta(e, f_2) = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{e} \cdot \left( \frac{1 - e^2}{2 - 2 \cdot f_2} - 1 \right) \right)$true_anomaly_e_f2
Zenith angle$\beta(\textbf{v}) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}}{v_3} \right)$zenith_angle_v
Symbols

Note that the bold letter $\textbf{v}$ denotes the velocity vector. In these cases the program assumes that the vector is three-dimensional.

Calculates the azimuth angle of the launch velocity vector v.

\[ \alpha(\textbf{v}) = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{v_2}{\sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}} \right)\]

  • $\alpha$: azimuth angle
  • $\textbf{v} = \left[ v_1, v_2, v_3 \right]^T$: velocity vector
